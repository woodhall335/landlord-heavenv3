7. CIRCUMSTANCES WHEN DEPOSIT MAY BE RETAINED

The landlord or agent may make deductions from the deposit for: 1. Unpaid Rent Any rent or other money owed under the tenancy agreement at the end of the tenancy. 2. Damage Damage to the property, furnishings or contents beyond normal wear and tear. This includes: - Broken items - Burns, cuts or stains beyond normal wear - Damage caused by pets - Holes in walls - Broken fixtures or fittings Normal wear and tear is NOT covered - this means reasonable deterioration from ordinary living. 3. Cleaning Professional cleaning costs if the property is returned in an unreasonably unclean condition compared to the check-in inventory. 4. Missing Items Replacement costs for: - Lost keys - Missing items from inventory - Removed fixtures or fittings 5. Other Breaches Costs arising from other breaches of the tenancy agreement.

Evidence Required

The landlord must provide evidence of any claimed deductions: - Check-in and check-out inventories - Photographs - Invoices for repairs/cleaning - Receipts - Expert reports if necessary

Both parties should: Step 1: Inspect the property together (check-out) Step 2: Compare condition to check-in inventory Step 3: Discuss and agree any deductions Step 4: Complete deposit return form Step 5: Submit agreed allocation to scheme The landlord will return your share within 10 working days of agreement.

9. DISPUTE RESOLUTION

If You Cannot Agree

If you and the landlord cannot agree on deposit deductions: Step 1: Try to negotiate - Communicate in writing - Exchange evidence (photos, receipts) - Consider compromise Step 2: Use Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) Both parties can ask the scheme to resolve the dispute using their free, impartial ADR service.

ADR Process

1. Either party raises a dispute with the scheme 2. Both parties submit evidence (photos, inventory, receipts, statements) 3. Independent adjudicator reviews all evidence 4. Decision made (usually within 28 days) 5. Scheme releases deposit according to decision 6. Decision is binding on both parties

ADR Service Details

Service: DPS ADR Service Contact: See scheme website Email: See scheme website Website: https://www.depositprotection.com/ Cost: FREE to both parties Time limit: You must raise a dispute within the deadline set by your deposit scheme of the tenancy ending (or when dispute becomes apparent).

Court Action

Alternatively, either party can take the matter to court, but this will involve: - Court fees (approx £100-£300) - Possible legal costs - Longer time to resolve - Risk of costs order ADR is usually quicker, cheaper and easier.

12. FAIR WEAR AND TEAR

Definition: Deterioration from ordinary use over time, considering: - Length of tenancy - Number of occupants - Original condition - Quality of items Examples of Fair Wear: - Faded curtains from sunlight - Worn carpet in high traffic areas - Minor scuffs on walls - Slight marks on worktops - Loosening of joints from normal use NOT Fair Wear (Damage): - Large holes in walls - Carpet burns or major stains - Broken furniture - Cracked tiles - Smashed fittings - Major uncleanliness - Pet damage Disputes over fair wear and tear are common - keep photos from check-in!

13. BETTERMENT

Landlords cannot charge for "betterment" - leaving property in better condition than at start. Example: If carpet was 5 years old at check-in and worn when you leave, landlord can only charge for proportion of replacement cost, not a brand new carpet.
