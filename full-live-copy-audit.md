## Executive Summary

The main copy problem is **not legal clarity**. It is that too many pages sound like they were written from the inside out: they describe internal routes, files, packs, and workflows instead of speaking naturally to a landlord who has a specific problem and wants a clear next step.

Across the site, three things happen repeatedly:

1. Pages use **internal language** like `route`, `file`, `pack`, `next practical step`, and rigid explainer labels instead of plain landlord-facing English.
2. Pages often speak about **the landlord in the third person** instead of speaking directly to **you**, which makes the copy feel stiff and less human.
3. Paragraphs frequently **make technical sense but do not read naturally**. They over-explain, repeat, caveat too heavily, or use templated transitions like `in practical terms` and `this guide explains`.

The weakest families are the **eviction and possession pages**, **tenancy pages**, and **product sales pages**. Ask Heaven performs best overall, but its wrapper still needs a more natural voice.

## Top Shared Copy Problems

1. **Internal route language** is everywhere, especially on eviction, money claim, tenancy, and hub pages.
2. **Third-person landlord phrasing** makes many pages sound stiff instead of direct.
3. **Product-first pack language** often takes priority over the landlord's real task.
4. **Template explainer labels** like `What it is` / `What it does` / `Why it is needed` flatten the copy and stop it sounding human.
5. **Formulaic blog wrapper language** slows the article before the answer begins.
6. **Ask Heaven's wrapper** sounds more robotic than the underlying answers deserve.
7. **Sample preview labelling** (`Sample pack proof`) sounds internal, even where the feature itself is strong.
8. **Caveat-heavy phrasing** (`usually`) weakens confidence and flow on several legal/process pages.
9. **Some titles and hero stacks are mechanically messy**, including at least one visible formatting bug (`(England, )`).
10. **Too many pages could swap headlines and subheads with each other** without sounding obviously wrong, which is a page-fit problem as much as a tone problem.

## Highest-Leverage Shared Template Fixes

- **`HighIntentPageShell` / `EvictionIntentLandingPage`**: remove `route` as the default noun; answer the landlord's problem directly; stop using `in practical terms`, `next practical step`, and similar templated transitions as structural crutches.
- **`EnglandTenancyPage` and related tenancy shells**: stop describing tenancy choices as routes; describe the real letting arrangement plainly; reduce third-person landlord phrasing.
- **`PublicProductSalesPage`**: replace rigid explainer labels with natural subheads; lead with the problem and outcome before naming the pack; rename `Sample pack proof`.
- **Blog wrapper**: cut `Read this first` and `This guide explains` style framing; keep CTA blocks in the article voice instead of dropping in product-routing copy.
- **Ask Heaven page wrapper**: shorten `You asked:` framing, lead with the direct answer, and move broad assistant/jurisdiction labels out of the primary answer path.

## Notes on Natural Language Review

This audit intentionally treated **natural language quality as a first-class problem**, not just a polish issue. A page was marked down not only when it was generic, but also when it:

- sounded like internal product or process language
- used awkward sentence construction
- stacked paragraphs in a way that felt stiff or illogical
- overused formulaic transitions and explainer labels
- or technically made sense while still not sounding like normal landlord-facing English

That is why so many pages fall into `Concern` or `Fail` even where the legal subject matter is correct. The biggest problem is often **voice and structure**, not factual substance.
