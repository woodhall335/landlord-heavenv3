# USA Upgrade

## Why this upgrade

This is a working internal brief for a U.S. rollout that sits alongside the current UK-first system without affecting existing public products or wizard flows.

The opportunity is strongest if Landlord Heaven positions the offer as a **state-specific self-file eviction pack**, not a generic notice generator. The product only feels commercially strong if it helps landlords move from notice to filing with state-specific court paperwork, service support, and practical filing guidance.

## Recommended 5-state launch

Launch states:

- California
- Texas
- Florida
- Georgia
- North Carolina

Launch assumptions:

- product type: `state-specific self-file eviction pack`
- launch price: `$69`
- rollout gate: env-only
- strongest full-pack states: California, Florida, North Carolina
- more locally variable states: Texas, Georgia

## Product promise

Recommended positioning:

> Prepare your notice, filing documents, service paperwork, and court-ready self-file pack for your state from one guided workflow.

The product should be sold as a landlord self-file system, not just a notice tool. That means it should cover the common pre-filing and filing-stage documents landlords need, while honestly separating court-issued items and local conditional items.

## State form-mapping approach

Every state pack should classify documents into these four buckets:

### Generated by us

- eviction notices
- service records / proof of service
- arrears schedules / rent ledgers
- lease-breach particulars
- landlord filing checklists
- filing instructions

### Filled from official form

- court complaint / petition
- summons where standardized
- cover sheets where standardized
- default / judgment forms where standardized
- military-status / nonmilitary affidavit where standardized

### Court-issued / not generated

- clerk-issued summonses where the court issues them directly
- hearing and trial settings
- entered judgments
- writ issuance and enforcement scheduling

### Local conditional add-on

- county / precinct / borough-specific forms
- venue-specific cover sheets
- local filing packet requirements
- local e-filing instructions and portal differences

- summons path
- default / military affidavit path
- writ / possession guidance

## Pricing and value

### Recommended price

- flat launch price: `$69`

### Value-for-money conclusion

`$69` is defendable only if the product is a real self-file pack.

That means the user should get more than a notice:

- notice
- complaint / petition
- summons
- service paperwork
- default / judgment support where applicable
- filing guidance
- local court guidance where required

If the product were reduced to mostly notice templates and generic instructions, `$69` would feel weak. If it includes the actual self-file stack and preview-before-payment experience, the value proposition is much stronger.

## Competition

Main competition types:

- free notice-template sites
- landlord document subscription platforms
- state court self-help pages and DIY interview tools

Notable competitor categories:

- free template libraries such as eForms and Avail
- subscription legal-doc platforms such as Rocket Lawyer
- landlord-form platforms such as ezLandlordForms
- official court/self-help resources in states like California, Florida, and North Carolina

The product is not unique enough if sold as a plain eviction notice generator. It becomes more differentiated if sold as a **state-specific self-file eviction pack** with guided outputs and filing support.

## Risks / next steps

Main risks:

- local court variation, especially in Texas and Georgia
- overpromising universal self-file coverage before local packet rules are mapped
- underdelivering on the court-pack promise while pricing at `$69`

Recommended next steps:

1. Build the U.S. system as a separate env-gated product lane.
2. Prioritize California, Florida, and North Carolina as the strongest full-pack states.
3. Add Texas and Georgia with stronger venue-aware filing guidance.
4. Build golden/sample packs for all 5 states before launch.
5. Keep New York out of wave 1.
